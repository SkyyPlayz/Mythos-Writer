The key was warm when Wren Ashby closed her fingers around it, warmer than it had any right to be after sitting in a solicitor's envelope for three days. Brass, old-fashioned, with a notch worn into the bow that her thumbnail fit perfectly, as if the metal had been waiting for exactly that thumb.

"The property transfers to you unconditionally," said Mr. Hartwick, without looking up from his papers. "Your grandmother left no conditions attached to the cottage or the — ah — associated business." He said *associated business* the way you might say *slightly embarrassing medical complaint*: with professional neutrality and a faint desire not to dwell.

Wren pocketed the key. "I know what the business is, Mr. Hartwick."

"Quite." He still didn't look up. "There is also the matter of the familiar."

The small iron teapot on his desk made a sound like a kettle beginning to boil, though the office was cold and there was no heat source Wren could identify.

"That would be Pip," she said.

"It has been waiting here since Thursday. It has been —" He paused and chose his word carefully. "Expressive."

Pip rotated ninety degrees on the desk. The spout pointed toward Wren with unmistakable intention.

She picked the teapot up. It was heavier than it looked and radiated warmth like a held hand. "Hello," she said quietly.

The teapot said nothing, because teapots don't speak. But something in its weight shifted, settled — satisfied, was the word that occurred to her, though she knew it was foolish to anthropomorphize a kitchen implement. Even one that had apparently been terrorizing a solicitor's office for four days.

Mr. Hartwick slid a folder across the desk. "The shop inventory, current accounts, and a letter your grandmother left for you personally. I was instructed to give it to you only after you accepted the key."

"What if I hadn't accepted?"

He looked up at last. "She said you would. She was, I understand, seldom wrong about such things."

Wren tucked Pip under her arm — the teapot nestled with suspicious neatness against her ribs — and opened the letter. Her grandmother's handwriting was cramped and emphatic, as if every word had been argued into existence.

*The shop runs itself*, it read, *more or less. The hearth knows what it's doing. Trust Pip, even when Pip is being insufferable — which is most of the time. The village knows you're coming. Be warm. The magic will follow the warmth, always.*

Outside the solicitor's window, a light rain was beginning over Ashford Village. Wren folded the letter and put it in her pocket alongside the key.

She had given up a graduate scholarship in Edinburgh, two months of lease left on a flat she could no longer afford, and a carefully constructed plan for a life in which nothing unexpected was supposed to happen. She had come here instead, to a cottage tea shop at the edge of a small Cotswold village, carrying a key that was warm and a teapot that had opinions.

She'd had worse ideas. Probably.

"All right," she said to Pip. "Let's go see what we've inherited."

The teapot made that small kettle-sound again — low, satisfied, the sound of water just beginning to think about boiling — and Wren Ashby walked out into the rain to begin her life as the witch of Ashford Village.
