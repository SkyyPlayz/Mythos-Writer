The shop opened, as far as Wren could tell, entirely on its own initiative.

She had meant to spend the morning taking inventory — her grandmother had left behind fourteen varieties of loose-leaf tea, a ledger in a handwriting she was still learning to decipher, and a chalk-board menu that updated itself while she wasn't looking. But at eight o'clock exactly, the front door swung open, the bell above it rang three times (the third time very insistently), and Elder Mossfoot came in stamping mud from his boots.

He was a small man, broad and weathered, with the particular solidity of someone who had been in the same place for so long that the place had grown around him. He carried a cloth cap he wouldn't put back on his head indoors and a problem he evidently couldn't put down at all.

"You'll be Rowan's girl," he said, settling into the chair nearest the hearth as if he'd done it a thousand times. He probably had. "You have the look of her around the eyes. Less grey, obviously."

"Wren." She hung her apron — which had appeared on the hook by the kitchen door sometime in the night — and put the kettle on. Or tried to. Pip was already on the hob, steaming gently. "What can I get you?"

"Something for a bad back would be appreciated, but I expect you'll say that's Sister Marigold's territory."

"It is," Wren agreed. "Tea, though — I can do tea."

"Chamomile. Your grandmother always knew which kind." He turned the cap over in his hands. "I've a problem, Miss Ashby. Not the back. Something stranger."

Pip produced chamomile of its own accord. Wren had already stopped being surprised by this. She carried the cup to Elder Mossfoot and sat across from him, because that, apparently, was what you did.

"Old Fenwick," the elder said, wrapping both hands around the cup. "You know the miller?"

"I know *of* him. Grandmother mentioned him in her notes."

"He forgets things. Not the ordinary kind of forgetting — not names and dates. He forgets the same hour, every day. Three in the afternoon. Everything that happens in that hour just — goes. He wakes up after it not knowing where the time went." Mossfoot looked at her over the rim of the cup. "Your grandmother was looking into it before she passed. Said it had the feel of something intentional."

Wren thought of her grandmother's letter. *The magic will follow the warmth.* She thought of Pip rotating on the solicitor's desk. She thought of the chalk-board, which currently read *Chamomile for trust and beginnings* in handwriting she recognized as her own, though she hadn't written it.

"How long has it been happening?" she asked.

"Six months. Maybe seven. It doesn't hurt him, not exactly — but there's things happening in that hour that he ought to know about. Things that concern the mill." Mossfoot set down his cup. "I know you're new. I know you've just come into the place. But Rowan trusted me with her problems, and I trusted her with mine, and —" He stopped, reformulating. "You're her granddaughter. That means something in Ashford. At least, I hope it does."

Through the window, Wren could see the village street beginning to fill with the slow purposeful movement of morning: a woman with a basket, two children arguing over something small and important, the butcher opening his shutters. A village she had never lived in, in a life she hadn't planned.

"Tell me everything you know about Fenwick's mill," she said. "And have another cup. There's no charge for the first visit."

Elder Mossfoot smiled for the first time, and the lines of his face rearranged themselves into something that looked, unexpectedly, like relief.

Behind Wren, Pip let out a long, satisfied sigh of steam.
