The problem arrived in the form of Delphine Coldwater, who knocked exactly twice and came in without waiting, which Wren was already learning meant she had something difficult to say and wanted to get it over with.

Delphine was tall, flour-dusted, with the bearing of someone who had been proved right about most things and found it more burden than comfort. Her bakery was three doors down from the tea shop — the best pasties in the county, according to Elder Mossfoot, though he'd added *not that I'm suggesting you try to compete* in a tone that suggested he was very much suggesting exactly that.

"I want to be clear," Delphine said, before Wren could offer her a seat, "that I don't believe in any of this."

"Any of what?" Wren asked pleasantly.

"*This.*" Delphine gestured at the room: the chalk-board, the self-updating menu, Pip, the hearth that burned with a fire nobody had lit. "Your grandmother was a kind woman and I respected her, but I always thought the magical bit was —" She stopped, choosing a word. "Theatrical."

"That's fair," Wren said. "What can I do for you?"

Delphine's jaw tightened fractionally. She was not a woman used to being wrong-footed by reasonableness. "My starter's dead. Fourteen-year-old sourdough culture, just — dead. Overnight. I've had bad batches before but not like this. It doesn't smell wrong, doesn't look wrong, it's just —" She paused. "Gone. Like the life went out of it."

"When did you notice?"

"This morning. The bread I baked yesterday was fine. But the starter I'm keeping for tomorrow's batch is just — nothing. I've got three standing orders for Saturday market."

Wren looked at Pip. Pip had turned to face Delphine with something that, in a person, would have been polite interest. In a teapot, it was harder to interpret, but Wren was getting better at it.

"Sourdough starter is alive," Wren said carefully. "Like any living thing, it can be — affected by certain disruptions in the local — um —"

"Magical field?" Delphine said it flatly, as if she were quoting something she didn't quite believe. "Your grandmother said the same thing once. She said there was a resonance in the village, centred on the hearth in this shop, and that when the resonance got disturbed, small domestic things would go wrong. Bread wouldn't rise. Butter wouldn't come. Candles wouldn't light." She sat down, without having been invited, and pressed her palms against the table. "I still don't believe it."

"But here you are," Wren said.

"But here I am." A long pause. "I can't afford to lose Saturday's orders."

Wren turned to the shelf behind her, running her fingers along the labelled jars until she found the one she wanted. Fennel seed and something she didn't have a name for yet — the label said *re-seeding*, in her grandmother's handwriting. She brought it down.

"Make a new culture tonight," she said, "with some of this mixed into the flour and water. Don't think of it as starting over. Think of it as the old one picking up where it left off."

Delphine looked at the jar with the expression of someone doing calculations they deeply resented. "And that works?"

"Grandmother had a seventeen-year-old culture going when she died. I think it works."

Delphine stood, took the jar, and didn't say thank you — but at the door she stopped.

"The sourdough was my mother's," she said, without turning around. "I know it's just bacteria and water. But."

"I know," Wren said. "It's still a loss."

The door closed. Behind Wren, Pip let out a sound very much like a sigh.

*The magic will follow the warmth*, her grandmother had written. Wren was beginning to understand that warmth didn't only mean temperature.
