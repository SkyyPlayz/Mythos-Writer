Old Fenwick did not know he forgot things, which was the cruelest part of it.

He arrived at the tea shop at eleven in the morning, as Elder Mossfoot had arranged, and he was a man of such straightforward good cheer that Wren spent the first ten minutes wondering if the elder had got the wrong person entirely. Fenwick was red-cheeked and round-shouldered, with flour in the creases of his knuckles and a laugh that arrived several seconds before the joke that prompted it. He shook Wren's hand with both of his and accepted tea and a slice of apple cake that Pip had produced from somewhere, and he talked about the mill with the easy precision of a man who knew his craft entirely and thought about it almost constantly.

"Four generations," he said, around a mouthful of cake. "Mill's been in the family since 1847. You'd think stone would get tired of grinding after that long, but it doesn't seem to mind." He paused, tilting his head. "Though now you mention it — ah." He stopped.

"Now I mention what?" Wren asked. She hadn't mentioned anything.

"Nothing, nothing." He shook his head with the cheerful bafflement of a man who couldn't find where he'd put down his keys. "Thought I had something, but it — well. It's gone now." He laughed, the big warm laugh arriving in the room. "Happens more these days. I expect it's just age."

It wasn't age. Wren could feel it — or rather, she couldn't feel it, which was the problem. When she sat with Elder Mossfoot, with Delphine, even with Juniper Stonewall who'd stopped in yesterday to introduce himself and eye Pip with professional suspicion — she could feel the warmth-resonance her grandmother had described, the particular aliveness of a person with their full self present. Sitting with Fenwick was like sitting with someone in a room where one window had been bricked over. Everything else was fine, the light was good, and you wouldn't notice unless you knew what you were looking for.

"Can I ask you something about the mill?" she said. "Something specific?"

"Course you can."

"What happens at three o'clock in the afternoon? The daily three?"

A brief, fractional pause. The laugh this time was different — still warm, still his, but with something slightly mechanical about the timing. "Three o'clock," he said. "I take a break, usually. Cup of tea. Watch the wheel for a bit. Peaceful time of day." He looked at her with eyes that were sharp and kind and missing something. "Why do you ask?"

"I'm trying to understand the mill's rhythm," Wren said, which was true, as far as it went. "I want to understand what a normal day looks like."

This was also true. But what she was really listening for was the sound of the gap — the way the story closed too smoothly around three o'clock, the way Fenwick's memory met something it couldn't see and flowed around it like water around a stone.

Something had placed a door in his mind at three in the afternoon and locked it from the outside.

The question was who — and what was happening in that hour that someone wanted him not to know about.

After he left, Wren sat with Pip by the hearth and thought about locked doors. The fire burned lower, and in the coals she thought she could see, just for a moment, the shape of her grandmother's face.

"I know," she said, to the coals. "I'm going to figure it out."

The fire settled with a sound like agreement.
