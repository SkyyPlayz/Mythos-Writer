Saturday market was where Wren learned that a village is not a collection of houses but a collection of relationships, and that she had inherited a position in most of them whether she'd asked to or not.

She had set up a small table at the edge of the market green — Pip on a trivet, a small folding sign that read *Hearthstone Tea & Consultations (by appointment or by need)* — mostly because her grandmother's ledger suggested she had always done so, and Wren was becoming increasingly convinced that following her grandmother's lead was the safest approach to most things.

By ten o'clock she had sold eleven sachets of tea, answered three questions about whether her grandmother had left any particular notes about particular problems (she had, for all three), and been assessed with frank curiosity by approximately half the village.

Thessaly Briarbend stopped at eleven-fifteen, ostensibly to buy lavender tea and actually, Wren was fairly certain, to talk about the mill.

"I'm the village bookkeeper," Thessaly said, holding the sachet and not looking at it. She was a narrow woman of forty or so, with the habit of sitting very still and the eyes of someone who had counted a great many numbers and noticed when they didn't add up. "I keep the accounts for most of the businesses here, including Fenwick's mill."

"How long have you been doing that?" Wren asked.

"Fifteen years." Thessaly paused. "For the past seven months, every Saturday morning when I go through the week's mill accounts, there's a gap. A transaction I can see in the bank records but can't match to any invoice, any delivery, any customer order. Exactly the same time stamp, every week. Three in the afternoon, Thursdays."

Wren felt the warmth in her chest shift — the particular shimmer that her grandmother's notes called *resonance recognition*, which was a fancy way of saying *pay attention*. "Do you know what the money is for?"

"No. And the strange thing is —" Thessaly looked around, the way people do when they're about to say something they half-believe and mostly don't. "Whenever I ask Fenwick about it, he can't remember. He goes over the figures with me perfectly well and then when we get to that entry, he just —" She made a small gesture. "Moves past it. As if it isn't there."

"As if he can't see it?"

"As if it isn't interesting enough to bother with." Thessaly met her eyes. "Your grandmother thought someone was using a compulsion. Something that made Fenwick look away from a specific thing at a specific time. She said she needed to identify the source before she could unpick it."

"Did she have any theories?"

"One." Thessaly picked up her tea with the deliberateness of someone committing to a decision. "She thought it might be connected to Sable Nightshade's visits. The merchant. Nightshade passes through every two or three months, always on a Thursday."

Wren watched Thessaly walk away toward the bookkeeper's stall, thinking about compulsions and Thursday afternoons and a miller who forgot the same hour every week.

Pip, on its trivet, rotated slowly until its spout pointed toward the far end of the market, where a tall figure in a dark coat was browsing the herb seller's table with the particular attention of someone who knew exactly what they were looking for.

Sable Nightshade had dark eyes and a way of being still that was different from Thessaly's — not careful, but watchful, the way a well-fed cat is watchful. When the merchant looked up and caught Wren's eye across the crowded green, something passed between them that was not quite a smile and not quite a challenge.

Pip made a small, quiet sound, like water that hadn't decided whether to boil.

"I see them," Wren said quietly. "I see them."
