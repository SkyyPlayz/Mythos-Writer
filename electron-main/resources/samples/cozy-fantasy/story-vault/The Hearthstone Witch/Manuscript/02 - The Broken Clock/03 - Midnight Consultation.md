Sable Nightshade came to the tea shop at midnight, because of course they did.

Wren had been half-expecting it — Pip had spent the evening sitting in the window, which it never did, and the fire had burned with the particular restless quality of a fire that knows company is coming. When the knock came, three soft raps with no particular urgency, Wren answered the door in her dressing gown with a reasonable degree of composure.

"Miss Ashby," said Sable Nightshade. "I apologise for the hour. I leave Ashford at first light and I don't like to leave unfinished conversations."

"We haven't started a conversation."

"No," Nightshade agreed, "but I think we both know we need to." They stood in the doorway, dark coat over dark clothing, and they were carrying a leather case that was clearly not a sample case, because no sample case was ever that carefully latched. "May I come in?"

"Pip?" Wren said.

The teapot on the kitchen table said nothing, but its lid lifted fractionally and dropped — a gesture Wren had come to read as *proceed but stay alert*.

She stepped aside.

Nightshade came in and sat without being invited, which in Wren's limited experience was either the mark of someone very comfortable or someone who didn't expect to be turned away, and settled the leather case on the table with both hands flat on the lid. Not opening it. Just — indicating it.

"I've been doing business with Conal Fenwick for six years," Nightshade said. "A private arrangement — materials he processes at the mill, payments made in cash, nothing that harms anyone. When your grandmother first approached me about the forgetting, I was — cooperative. We worked together for three months. Then she passed."

"You knew she was investigating?"

"She asked me directly." Nightshade looked at Wren with the measuring look of a person recalibrating something. "I don't respond well to indirection. Your grandmother understood that." A pause. "The forgetting wasn't my doing. I've been paying him for a legal private arrangement, and I've been as unsettled as anyone that he can't remember it."

"Then who is responsible for the compulsion?"

"I have a theory." Nightshade unlatched the case. Inside, wrapped in oil-cloth, was a single object: a pocket watch, old-fashioned, its face cracked through with a line that cut across the three o'clock mark exactly. "I found this six weeks ago, near the mill's north wheel. I know what it is. I think you will too."

Wren leaned forward. The watch was cold — not room-temperature cold but the specific cold of something that had had warmth deliberately removed from it, the way a hearth-space feels after a fire has been quenched in anger. The resonance she felt from it was sour, off-key, like a beautiful song played on an instrument that had been tuned wrong on purpose.

"That's not just a watch," she said.

"No," Nightshade said. "It's an anchor. And someone's been winding it every Thursday afternoon, which is why Fenwick forgets every Thursday afternoon, and why whoever's winding it doesn't want him to see."

The fire behind Wren popped once, sharply, and in the coals, just for a moment, her grandmother's face was very clear.

"Keep the watch," Wren said. "Don't let it out of your sight. I need to find out who's winding it before I can break what's been done to Fenwick." She straightened up. "You'll have to trust me."

"Your grandmother earned my trust," Nightshade said, latching the case carefully. "That doesn't extend to her granddaughter automatically."

"Fair enough." Wren looked at Pip. Pip's spout was aimed squarely at Nightshade, steadily, the way a compass needle points north. "But I notice you came here anyway."

Something shifted in Nightshade's careful face — not quite a smile, but the space where a smile might appear. "Your grandmother said you were perceptive." They stood, picking up the case. "She also said you'd need more confidence in your own instincts. First night in the shop and you've already identified the mechanism of a compulsion curse that had her stumped for a week." A brief pause at the door. "Do think about that."

After they left, Wren sat by the fire for a long time, and the flames burned steady and warm, and she thought about locked watches and unlocked hours and what kind of person winds a clock in secret every single Thursday.

"Right," she said finally. "I suppose we're proper detectives now."

Pip made the kettle-sound — low, certain, beginning-to-boil — and the night settled in around the shop like a familiar placed its head in its owner's lap.
