Sister Marigold was at the mill when Wren arrived, as expected. The healer looked up from a small work-bench near the north wheel and, to her credit, did not flinch.

"I wondered how long it would take," Marigold said. She set down the tools she'd been using — delicate things, a healer's tools, the kind for very fine work. "Your grandmother was very good. She got within a week of knowing before she passed. I half-thought she'd decided to let it go."

"She left it for me," Wren said. "Not as a burden — as something to understand." She stayed near the door, because this was not a confrontation, even if it felt like one. "Tell me about the watch."

Marigold folded her hands. She was in her fifties, broad-shouldered, with the posture of someone who had spent decades helping people stand upright and had never learned to do it for herself. "Conal's mother had an illness. Twelve years ago. Palsy, but a specific kind — it's called the forgetting tremor, and it comes in waves. She had it badly and she was terrified of what it would do to her son. She knew she was dying and she asked me to give him something, when she went, that would make the end easier." A pause. "The watch was her grandmother's. A hearth-anchor, very old. She thought I should use it."

"To take away his grief," Wren said.

"To take away one hour a week," Marigold said carefully. "The Thursday at three — the time she died, and the time he always used to visit her. I removed that hour from his grief, just that hour, so he wouldn't have to feel it." Her voice was steady. "It wasn't meant to be permanent. I thought — a year, maybe two, and then I'd let it fade and he'd have healed enough. But twelve years is a long time, and he — it had become part of him by then. The forgetting. I was afraid to take it away."

Wren was quiet for a moment. Somewhere in the mill, the wheel turned and the water moved.

"That's not your choice to make," she said finally. "It wasn't her choice to make for him either, I'm sorry. Grief isn't a wound that needs covering. It's part of knowing someone."

Marigold closed her eyes briefly. "I know. I've known for at least eight years." She opened them. "I kept telling myself I'd stop soon. And then I didn't."

"Will you let me break it?"

"Can you?"

"I think so." Wren looked at the watch on the work-bench — the cracked face, the frozen three. "It'll take time. It'll need to be gentle, because he'll remember everything at once otherwise, and that's — that's not how you give grief back to someone. You do it slowly, like sunlight coming back after a long winter." She picked up the watch carefully, feeling the cold misalignment of it in her palm. "He's going to feel it. He's going to grieve his mother again, properly, for the first time."

"I know." Marigold's voice was rough. "That's what I've been doing wrong for twelve years. Thinking I was protecting him from the pain when I was just delaying it." She met Wren's eyes. "I'll tell him the truth. What I did, why I did it, and that I'm sorry. He deserves to know."

"Yes," Wren said. "He does."

She walked home through the early evening of Ashford Village with the watch in her pocket and Pip warm against her ribs and a complicated tangle of feelings she was learning not to rush through. The village moved around her — Delphine closing up the bakery, Tobias running an errand for Thessaly, Elder Mossfoot on his usual evening circuit — and Wren understood for the first time that this was what her grandmother had been: not a solver of problems, but a holder of them. A hearth-keeper. Someone who made it possible for people to bring their most difficult truths out of the cold and into the warmth, where they could be seen properly.

The shop was warm when she got back, the fire burning even and steady. Pip went to its place on the hob and settled there with a sound of deep, absolute satisfaction.

On the chalk-board, in her grandmother's handwriting, one final note:

*You did well, Wren. You'll do better. The village is lucky.*

She stood there for a long moment, reading it.

Then she put the kettle on, because the magic follows the warmth, and the warmth always starts with tea.
