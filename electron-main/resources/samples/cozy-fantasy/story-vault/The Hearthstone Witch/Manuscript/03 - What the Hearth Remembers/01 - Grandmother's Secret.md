The face in the fire had been trying to speak for three days before Wren finally sat still long enough to listen.

She had been busy — identifying the watch's maker (a name in her grandmother's ledger, circled twice), cross-referencing with Thessaly's accounts, teaching young Tobias the first principles of hearth-reading, dealing with a minor crisis when Juniper Stonewall came in to ask, very professionally, whether she knew anything about a shopkeeper on the edge of town whose bread had started singing. She had not been sitting still. She was not, by nature, a sitting-still kind of person.

But on Thursday afternoon — exactly at three — she sat.

She didn't plan to. She had been mid-cup at the kitchen table when a quality of stillness entered the room that was simply too pointed to ignore, the kind of stillness that felt like a held breath, and she'd looked at the hearth and her grandmother had been there.

Not a ghost. Or not exactly. More like a memory that had been waiting for the right conditions to play. The fire shaped itself into a face she knew from childhood, patient and a little exasperated and very clearly about to say something important.

"Grandmama," Wren said, very quietly. "Hello."

The fire-face moved. No sound — this wasn't a speaking, it was a showing. The coals rearranged to show a room she didn't recognise: the mill, from the inside, at three o'clock on a Thursday, with the sound of the wheel and the light falling through the high window. And in the corner, hands moving over a small clock with the precision of someone who had done this many times, a person she did recognise: Sister Marigold.

Wren's cup stopped halfway to her lips.

Sister Marigold was the village healer. She had come into the shop twice, pragmatic and cheerful, full of useful knowledge about medicinal herbs and the good honest common sense of a woman who had no patience for nonsense. She had seemed completely, unambiguously fine.

She had also been in the mill every Thursday at three, apparently, winding a watch that made Old Fenwick forget she was there.

The fire-memory continued. Wren watched her grandmother watch Marigold, watched the realisation move across the old woman's face not as surprise but as sorrow — the particular sorrow of finding out something you'd half-known and half-refused to know. And then the fire settled, the coals quieted, and her grandmother was gone.

Wren sat with her cooling tea and thought about motivations. Marigold was the healer. She had access to Fenwick's mill on legitimate grounds — he had a recurring knee problem, Wren had seen the notes. What did Marigold want that she could only get on Thursday afternoons, in secret, in the mill?

What was she taking? Or leaving? Or doing?

Pip turned slowly on the table, spout pointing toward the door — but not toward the village. Toward the path that led down to the mill.

"You think I should go," Wren said.

The teapot said nothing.

"You're right that I should go." She stood, finished her tea, and began wrapping her shawl. "This isn't an accusation, though. I'm going to give Marigold the chance to explain, because Grandmother watched her and came home sad, not angry, and there's a difference."

She picked Pip up. The teapot was warm and solid against her chest.

"Whatever she's doing in that mill," Wren said, more quietly, "I think she's been doing it for a very long time. And I think it costs her something."

The fire burned low and steady behind her as she went out, and the warmth of the cottage followed her down the path — her grandmother's warmth, the accumulated warmth of every cup of tea ever brewed there, every problem heard and held — and Wren Ashby walked toward the truth the way she'd been walking toward everything since arriving in Ashford: carefully, openly, and with the strong conviction that whatever she found, it would make sense if she listened properly.
