The Wednesday Club had met every second Wednesday of the month for thirty-one years, in the back room of the Millhaven public library, and in all that time no member had ever simply not appeared.

Helena Marsh arrived at six fifty-five, which was five minutes before the official start and the same time she had arrived for every meeting since 1994. She hung her coat on the hook by the door, said hello to the librarian's assistant, and went through to the back room, where the chairs were arranged in a rough circle around a low table, the tea things were already set out, and Oliver Graves's chair was empty.

Oliver was never late. Oliver was, if anything, compulsively on time — a quality Helena had attributed to his years as a solicitor, where lateness was a form of disrespect and disrespect was a form of losing. He had been a member since 1997 and he had not missed a single meeting without advance notice in twenty-four years. She had a text from him the previous Thursday confirming his attendance for this week's discussion of *The Comfort of Strangers* — a book he'd been looking forward to, he said, because it was the kind of thing the Club needed to argue about.

She texted him at five past seven. No response.

She rang at a quarter past. No answer.

Beatrice Fenn — the Club's founder, sixty-nine, with the particular composure of a woman who had weathered many things by refusing to show that she found them difficult — suggested they begin without him. There were only four of them tonight, which already made the discussion feel thin. Frederick Tam arrived at twenty past and took the chair nearest the door, which was not his usual chair, and Helena noticed he did not take off his coat for the first quarter of an hour.

They talked about *The Comfort of Strangers*. Or they moved their mouths in the direction of talking about it. What they were actually doing, all four of them, was listening for a text or a footstep or some other sign that the explanation for Oliver's absence was ordinary.

At eight o'clock, Helena excused herself and walked to Oliver's flat on Harbour Street. It took seven minutes. The light in the first-floor window was on.

The building's entrance door was unlocked — Oliver had complained about this for years — and she went up the stairs and knocked on his door and waited.

No sound from inside. Not the shuffle of someone approaching, not the television, not the radio programme he always had on when he was home in the evening.

She tried the door. The handle turned.

Helena Marsh had taught English literature for thirty-eight years. She had read more detective novels than she could count, had been drawn to them for reasons she'd never fully examined, and had spent the last three decades knowing, in the abstract, that she had the kind of mind that would be useful in an investigation. She had never expected to apply it.

She pushed the door open.

The flat was neat — Oliver was always neat — and the light in the sitting room was on, and the book he'd been reading was open, face-down, on the arm of the chair, and Oliver Graves was on the floor between the chair and the window.

She didn't go in immediately. She stood in the doorway for a moment, taking in the details the way she'd trained herself over years of close reading: methodically, without allowing the first impression to determine the interpretation.

He was dead. She knew that before she reached him. The stillness was wrong in a particular way — not sleep, not illness. The stillness of something that had stopped.

She checked his pulse anyway, because that was the right thing to do, and then she stepped back to the doorway, took a breath, and rang the police.

What she noticed, while she waited, standing in the doorway that she was careful not to contaminate by touching: the book on the arm of the chair, face-down. The wine glass on the table, almost empty. The second wine glass on the table, almost full.

Oliver Graves had been expecting company.
