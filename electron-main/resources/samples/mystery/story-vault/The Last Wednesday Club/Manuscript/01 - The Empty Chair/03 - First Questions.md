She began with the book club, because the book club was what she knew.

Helena had spent the night reconstructing Oliver's last weeks from the limited material she had: his texts, their last three meetings, the things he'd said and — as importantly — the things he hadn't. By morning she had a list of observations she was not yet willing to call suspicions, on a notepad she'd taken from her desk at home.

*Old papers — mentioned 14th. Specific? Legal files? Personal?*
*Visitor on Wednesday. Not club member (all present). Who expected at 7pm?*
*Sonia Graves — when did she come back to Millhaven? Oliver mentioned her in October.*
*Frederick Tam. Coat on for first 15 minutes. Not his usual chair.*

That last one nagged. Frederick had been Oliver's physician for twelve years, and his nearest thing to a close friend in the club. He'd been distracted all evening — which Helena had attributed to Oliver's absence, because they all had been — but the coat and the wrong chair suggested something more like preparation: a man arranging himself for a quick exit.

She rang Beatrice Fenn at nine.

"I expect the police will want to speak with everyone," Beatrice said immediately. She had the voice of a woman who had already been through this conversation mentally and arrived at her positions. "I've been thinking about it since last night."

"What were you thinking?"

A silence, which with Beatrice usually indicated considered rather than unprepared. "Oliver was not himself for the past two months. I thought it was the anniversary — it's been fifteen years since his divorce, and he sometimes gets low in November. But low and anxious are different things." A pause. "He asked me, in September, whether I still had the minutes from the club's early years. The written minutes. I do — I've kept them since the beginning."

"Did he say why he wanted them?"

"He said he was writing something. A memoir of sorts, or notes toward one — he wasn't sure yet." Beatrice's voice was careful in a way Helena recognised as someone measuring what to share. "He borrowed three folders from 1995 and 1996. He returned them last month. I didn't look at whether he'd made copies."

Helena noted: *Club minutes 1995-96. What happened then?*

"Is there anything in those years you'd want to revisit?" Helena asked.

A pause that was slightly too long. "The club was quite small then. Four or five members, including Oliver and myself. We had — there were some difficulties with a member who left." Another pause. "I don't want to speculate, Helena."

"No," Helena agreed. "Neither do I." She looked at her notepad. "But Oliver was going through his papers, and he was anxious, and now he's dead with a poured drink for a visitor who didn't stay. Something was happening in the last few weeks that I think he didn't tell any of us."

Beatrice said, carefully: "No. He told someone."

"Who?"

"I don't know. But he told me, in our last telephone call, that he'd spoken to someone about it. Someone he trusted to handle it properly." She stopped. "I thought he meant a solicitor. Or the police, perhaps. But if he'd gone to the police and something happened that same evening —"

"Then whoever he was going to meet knew he'd spoken to someone."

Millhaven harbour was grey in the November morning, the boats rocking in a light chop, and Helena sat at her kitchen table with her cold cup of tea and her notepad and the particular feeling of a pattern not yet visible but already present, like a shape in fog.

She picked up the pen.

*Who did Oliver trust? And who didn't want him to tell them?*
