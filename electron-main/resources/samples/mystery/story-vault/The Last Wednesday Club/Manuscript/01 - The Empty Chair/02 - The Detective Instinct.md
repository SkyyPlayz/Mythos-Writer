Constable Whittaker was twenty-nine and professionally polite, which Helena had found, in the general course of life, to be the combination most reliably deployed when someone wanted to be helpful but found her difficult to categorise.

"You didn't touch anything else?" he said, for the third time, with the tone of someone who believed the answer but wanted her to understand that he was asking seriously.

"The door handle," Helena said. "Oliver's wrist. I've told you twice."

"Right." He wrote something in his notebook. "And you'd had no indication that Mr. Graves was unwell? In recent weeks?"

"He seemed entirely well." Helena was sitting on the stairs, because Whittaker had been firm about keeping her out of the flat, which was correct. "He was looking forward to the book club. He was planning a trip to Edinburgh in the spring. He had recently —" She paused, organizing. "He had recently been exercised about something, but I didn't have the impression it was health-related."

"Exercised."

"Anxious. Preoccupied. He mentioned, at our last meeting, that he'd been going through some of his old papers and had come across something he found unsettling. I didn't press, because Oliver — he'd tell you what he wanted to tell you, and pushing rarely helped."

"Do you know what papers?"

"No." She looked at the constable. "But the second wine glass on the table was almost full. He'd poured a drink for someone who either hadn't arrived or hadn't stayed long enough to drink it."

Whittaker looked at her for a moment with the expression of someone processing an unexpected data point. "We'll look into that." He wrote something. "Ms. Marsh, I want to be clear that this is likely to be a medical matter. Mr. Graves was seventy-three, and the preliminary indication —"

"He was in excellent health," Helena said. "His doctor — Frederick Tam, who is in our book club — would confirm that. And I don't think a man in excellent health who was expecting a visitor would die of natural causes on the same evening with his door unlocked and his visitor apparently absent."

A pause. Whittaker's pen was still.

"You found him," he said carefully. "That was clearly distressing."

"It was." She met his eyes steadily. "It was also informative. The sitting room was undisturbed except for the two glasses, one of which was almost full. The book he was reading had been placed down carefully, not dropped — the spine was maintained. Whatever happened to Oliver, it happened very quickly and possibly before his visitor arrived, or the visitor chose not to make themselves known afterward."

Whittaker looked at her for a long, quiet moment. He was working through something — she could see it — the mental adjustment required when a witness turns out to have already done more analysis than expected.

"Ms. Marsh," he said finally. "Have you done this before?"

"Read detective fiction for forty years," she said. "But no. Not this." She stood, because her knees were beginning to protest the stairs. "I know I'm not the investigating officer and I don't intend to interfere with your work. But I knew Oliver Graves for thirty-one years and I am not prepared to accept that he simply died before someone could tell me what he'd been anxious about. If you find that the wine glass and the papers and the unlocked door all have ordinary explanations, I will be very relieved." She looked at him steadily. "Do you expect that?"

A very long pause.

"No," Constable Whittaker said, in the tone of a man who would very much rather he did. "Not entirely."
