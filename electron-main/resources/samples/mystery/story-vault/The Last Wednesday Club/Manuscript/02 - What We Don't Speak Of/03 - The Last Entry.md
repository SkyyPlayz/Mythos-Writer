The document was in Oliver's handwriting — the cramped, precise hand of a man trained in legal drafting — on three pages folded inside the back cover of *The Comfort of Strangers*, the book that had been sitting face-down on the arm of his chair.

Whittaker found it on the second search of the flat, having now had cause to look more carefully. He brought it to Helena that evening, as a courtesy that was simultaneously an acknowledgment and a request.

"He wrote it that day," Whittaker said. "The morning of. If anything happened to him, he wanted it found."

Helena read it at her kitchen table with Sonia sitting across from her.

It was not a confession and not a will — it was a timeline, the kind a thorough solicitor would draft to make sure something could be reconstructed if he wasn't available to explain it himself. Dated. Cited. Three pages of evidence in precise order, with annotations indicating where the originals were held.

The trust had been established in Oliver's name without his knowledge in October 1995. The money — forty-two thousand pounds, accurately recalled by Sonia — had come from a dissolution of assets from the estate of a man named Peter Callaway, who had died intestate in 1994 and whose estate had been informally managed by his general practitioner during the period when the family was deciding what to do. The general practitioner had been Frederick Tam, three years into his Millhaven practice, two years before anyone would have thought to look too carefully at a young doctor's sideline in estate management.

The trust had been dissolved in 1997. The money had moved through two intermediaries. The end point was a private account held in the name of Beatrice Fenn.

Helena sat with the document for a long time without speaking.

"Beatrice and Frederick," Sonia said, very quietly.

"Forty-two thousand pounds in 1995 is —" Helena calculated. "Close to a hundred thousand now. And they've had thirty years with it." She set the document down. "Oliver found it in his files and he went to Frederick. And then he rang Beatrice — I don't know when. She told me he'd spoken to someone he trusted. She let me assume that meant a solicitor or the police." Helena looked at the document. "He may have rung Beatrice first. He'd known her for thirty years. He may have told her what he'd found before he showed Frederick."

"Which would have given them time," Sonia said.

"Yes." Helena looked at Whittaker, who had been standing quietly in the kitchen doorway with an expression that she was beginning to recognise as his listening face — not passive, but absorbing. "Oliver was a careful man. He wrote this document to make sure it could be found. He placed it somewhere he knew would eventually be searched properly." She looked at the book, *The Comfort of Strangers* still in its evidence bag. "He was expecting Frederick that evening. He'd poured a drink for him. But I think he also suspected that Frederick might not be coming alone, or might not be coming in good faith."

"He knew it might go wrong," Sonia said.

"He knew it might go wrong," Helena confirmed. "And he left a record anyway."

Whittaker took the document carefully. "Ms. Marsh," he said, and she knew from his voice that he was about to say something formal, official, necessary. "I need you to —"

"I know," she said. "I'll come to the station tomorrow and give a full statement." She looked at Sonia. "And I think Miss Graves should be present when you bring in Dr. Tam and Mrs. Fenn. Not because she needs to see it, but because Oliver wrote three pages to make sure his daughter knew the truth."

Sonia looked at her. She had her father's eyes, and in them Helena could see something she recognised from thirty-one years of book clubs and slow conversations and the particular trust of people who have known each other long enough to skip the preamble.

Oliver Graves had spent his last day making sure the truth would come out. The Wednesday Club, in the end, had done what it always did: sat with difficult things and argued about them until they yielded to something true.

Helena would write his obituary herself, she decided. She knew what to say.
