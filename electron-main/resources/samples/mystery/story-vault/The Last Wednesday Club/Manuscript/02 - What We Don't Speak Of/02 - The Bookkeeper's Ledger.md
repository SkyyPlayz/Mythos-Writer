Sonia Graves arrived on Saturday morning, a day early, and she came to Helena directly rather than to the police.

She was forty-one, with her father's eyes and her own manner — someone who had been making self-contained decisions for a long time and was in the habit of not explaining them. She sat at Helena's kitchen table and accepted tea and looked at the harbour through the window and said: "He told me. In October. That's why I came back."

Helena set down her cup. "Came back to Millhaven?"

"I've been here since the third of November. Staying at the Anchor." Sonia turned to face her. "He found documents. Old ones, from his practice — Oliver was a solicitor in Millhaven for thirty years, you know, before he retired. He kept meticulous files. And about three months ago, going through his old papers, he found something in the 1995-1996 files that he said shouldn't exist."

"What was it?"

"A financial instrument — a discretionary trust, set up in 1995 in my father's name as trustee. A large sum. He told me he had no memory of ever establishing it and no record of the client whose assets it was supposed to manage." Sonia's voice was precise, like Oliver's had been. "Someone had used my father's solicitor registration to set up a trust and then, apparently, dissolved it two years later. He couldn't work out how."

Helena thought of the financial records from 1995-96 in the partial folder. "He was being used," she said.

"He thought so. He thought his signature had been — not forged, but used. That someone with access to his files had used his name and credentials to establish a financial vehicle that he knew nothing about." A pause. "The money in the trust — the records he had were partial, but he'd worked out the amount. Forty thousand pounds in 1995. A significant sum. And it had come from somewhere he couldn't trace."

"Did he suspect anyone?"

Sonia was quiet for a moment. "He had a name," she said. "From the trust documents. A co-trustee, listed on the original instrument — someone who would have had to sign off on the establishment. He didn't want to tell me the name until he was certain." Another pause. "But he told me who he was going to show the documents to. Someone he trusted to verify them independently."

"Who?"

"Frederick Tam," Sonia said. "He rang Frederick on Wednesday afternoon. He was going to show him the documents that evening."

Helena looked at her notepad, sitting on the kitchen table. She thought about Frederick Tam arriving at the book club in his coat and choosing the wrong chair and leaving as soon as he reasonably could.

"Dr. Tam came to the book club," Helena said carefully. "He seemed preoccupied."

"I know." Sonia's hands around her teacup were very still. "I think Frederick Tam came to the book club because he needed to appear normal that evening. I think he knew, when he arrived, that Oliver was already dead." She met Helena's eyes. "I think he went to see my father, and I think something happened, and I think whatever Oliver had found implicates Frederick Tam."

The harbour was still and grey, and the kettle clicked off, and Helena sat with the particular feeling of a pattern becoming visible in the fog — shape first, then detail, then the clear terrible outline of something you had been hoping would resolve into something else.

"Tell me everything Oliver told you," she said. "All of it. From the beginning."
