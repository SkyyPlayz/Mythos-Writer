Whittaker let her in on Thursday, which she considered both surprising and a sign that his earlier assessment of her had been more favourable than his manner suggested.

"The family has been notified," he said, on the landing, with the air of a man preparing her for something. "His daughter will be here Saturday."

"Sonia," Helena said. "Yes." She'd heard Sonia Graves mentioned in club conversations over the years as a subject Oliver discussed with the particular combination of pride and complicated feeling that characterised his relationship with most things he cared about. She had not been to Millhaven in fifteen years, Oliver had said in October, and then said it in the tone of a man who did not want to discuss it further.

The flat was unchanged from Wednesday, which was both correct and slightly wrong — unchanged because it had been preserved, not because no one had disturbed it. Someone had been through it carefully. The drawers in the bedroom sat at slightly different angles than memory suggested, and the papers on the desk in the sitting room were less disordered than they'd been when she'd stood in the doorway two nights ago.

"Has anyone been here besides your team?" she asked.

"The scene was secured." Whittaker paused. "As far as we know."

*As far as we know* was a phrase Helena had heard before, in different contexts. It meant: *we believe this to be true but it's not a certainty I'm willing to commit to*.

"What killed him?" she asked.

"I'm not at liberty to —"

"Whittaker." She said it without reproach, just precisely. "You let me in. You have a reason. Tell me what you're looking for."

He looked at her for a moment. Then: "Cardiac event. Or something that presented identically. We're waiting on the toxicology." A pause. "The second glass had been washed and returned to the cabinet. By the time forensics arrived, there was no glass on the table at all."

Helena felt the particular chill of a detail that clarifies everything.

"So someone came back," she said. "After he died. Cleaned up the glass. And you want to know if there's anything here that Oliver might have been about to give to someone."

"We found a folder," Whittaker said. "In the desk drawer. It had been partly gone through — some pages removed, we think, because there are gaps in the numbering sequence. The pages that remain are photocopies of what look like financial records from 1995 and 1996."

"The club minutes covered the same period," Helena said. "He'd borrowed them from Beatrice."

"Yes." He looked at her steadily. "Ms. Marsh. I want to be clear that this is an active investigation and you are not —"

"I know what I am," she said. "But I've known Oliver Graves for thirty-one years, and whoever came back to clean that glass has better access to him than you do." She looked around the tidy flat with its old books and its careful arrangements, Oliver's whole life in eighty square metres, and felt the particular grief of looking at a person's absence in their presence. "He trusted people. Very specifically, very carefully. He told Beatrice he'd spoken to someone about what he found. He was expecting a visitor." She turned to face Whittaker. "I think his visitor came and went and cleaned up the evidence, and I think they did it because they knew Oliver well enough to know what to remove and where he kept things. That's not a stranger."

Whittaker wrote in his notebook for a long moment.

"Tell me about the 1995-1996 period," he said. "The club."

"I wasn't a member yet," Helena said. "I joined in 1998. But I know someone who was." She thought of Beatrice's careful pause. *There were some difficulties with a member who left.* "I think it's time I had a more specific conversation with Beatrice Fenn."
