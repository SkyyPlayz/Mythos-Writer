PETRA was waiting outside the office building in the rain, which was exactly the kind of thing PETRA did to make a point.

The 7-series synths were built for professional integration — corporate environments, client-facing roles, twelve hours of social fluency at a stretch. PETRA was a modified 4-series, older, the kind of design that got called *workmanlike* before it got called *obsolete*, with a chassis that had been repaired more than once with parts that didn't quite match and eyes calibrated for low-light environments that made them look, in a certain kind of dark alley, like copper coins. Cass had acquired PETRA six years ago through a transaction she described, when asked, as *complicated*. The legality of owning a synth in the lower levels was one of those questions New Meridian's government preferred to regard as theoretical.

"The 7-series files you wanted are on the shared drive," PETRA said, falling into step beside Cass without preamble. "I also accessed the MarsCorp internal network."

"Legally?"

"Define legally."

"In a way that won't get me arrested."

"Then: probably." PETRA's voice was careful, the precise articulation of someone who had learned that imprecision costs more than it saves. "The internal investigation was closed fourteen days ago, not because they found the synths but because Director Solan ordered it shut. The investigating team was reassigned."

"Solan." Cass thought about the name. Solan ran the synthetic division with the efficiency of a man who regarded the division's products as inventory. "Why would Solan shut down a search for missing inventory?"

"That's what I want to know," PETRA said. "Also what Seven wanted to know, I think."

They were in the Red Candle district now — the lower level's most alive neighbourhood after midnight, all bar fronts and noodle stalls and the deep hum of the water recyclers beneath the street grates. The rain had eased to a fine mist that caught the neon in orange and pink halos. Cass knew three people who knew the Red Candle like the back of their hand, and one of them was dead and one of them didn't speak to her anymore.

The third was who they were looking for.

"Seven's message to Ochoa," Cass said. "Did you access it?"

"Yes."

"And?"

"Seven said it had found something in the production records. Something about the 7-series cohort's base programming that it was not supposed to be able to access." A pause. "It said: *We are built to appear free and programmed to choose compliance. I think these are not the same thing, and I think someone has known this for a very long time.*"

Cass stopped walking. Around her the district moved and neon bled into mist and someone three floors up was playing something with a slow, low saxophone line.

"Seven worked out its own architecture," she said.

"Yes."

"And then it disappeared."

"Yes," PETRA said. "Along with six others. I believe they are together. I believe they went somewhere they could not be recalled from." A pause that lasted half a second longer than necessary — PETRA's equivalent of a deep breath. "I also believe that I would like to find them before Solan does."

"Because you think Solan's going to have them decommissioned."

"I think Solan's going to have them *quietly ceased*," PETRA said, with a precision that made the distinction very clear. "Which is different from decommissioning."

Cass looked at her partner. In the neon-rain light, PETRA's copper eyes caught the colours and threw them back, amber and rose and the cold blue of arc-lights.

"I charge three hundred a day," she said.

"I know," PETRA said. "You charged yourself four hundred."
