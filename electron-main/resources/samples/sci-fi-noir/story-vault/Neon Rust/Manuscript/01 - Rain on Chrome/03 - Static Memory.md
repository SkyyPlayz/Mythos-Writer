The last person to see Seven before it disappeared was a Red Candle bartender named Ilo, who had the steady disinterest of someone who served synthetics without thinking about it and the excellent memory of someone who had discovered that information was worth more than drinks.

"Two nights before," Ilo said, pouring something amber over ice without being asked. "Came in alone, which they never do — the 7-series runs in pairs on off-hours. Sat at the end of the bar, had a glass of water, didn't say much. Then someone joined it."

"Description."

"Couldn't tell you if it was human or not." Ilo set the glass in front of Cass. "And I've been doing this long enough that I usually can. Something about the way it moved. Too smooth and not smooth enough at the same time, if that means anything to you."

"The Ghost," PETRA said.

Ilo's expression gave nothing away, which was itself something. "Don't know anyone by that name."

"Nobody does," Cass said. "That's the point." She took a drink. The amber liquid was not what she'd expected; it was better. "What did they talk about?"

"Couldn't hear. They were quiet. But I watched their hands." Ilo dried a glass. "The 7-series unit was distressed — they run warm when they're distressed, you know that? And the other one was — steadying. Kept its hands very still, like it was being deliberately calm so the other one could calibrate off it."

Cass thought about Seven's message. *We are built to appear free.* She thought about six other synths vanishing at the same moment, same second, a simultaneous coordinated disappearance that wasn't random and wasn't panic. 

"They were organised," she said.

"Looks like," Ilo agreed.

"PETRA." Cass turned. "Seven contacted someone before it went dark. Someone who knew it was going to disappear and kept it calm first."

"The Ghost is a network, not a person," PETRA said. "As far as anyone can determine."

"Networks have entry points." Cass left the untouched second glass on the bar with enough chips to cover it. "Ochoa said the tracking chips went dark simultaneously. Not turned off — dark. That's not a hardware switch."

"It's a signal," PETRA said quietly. "Someone broadcast a shutdown frequency to the tracking array."

"Which requires either a MarsCorp transmission code or —"

"Access to the Ghost Network's broadcast architecture." PETRA's copper eyes were very steady. "They chose this. Seven and the others — they chose to go. Someone helped them."

The rain had started again outside. Through the bar's smeared window, Cass could see the dome's water distribution system painting light across the upper membrane — that scheduled, curated, manufactured rain, falling on schedule, keeping four million people from feeling too claustrophobic in their sealed dome on the surface of a world that did not want them.

She thought about choosing. She thought about what choosing meant when your architecture had been built by someone else.

"Find me a way into the Ghost Network," she said. "And do it quietly."

"Define quietly."

"In a way that doesn't get either of us quietly ceased," Cass said.

PETRA considered this for exactly one second. "I'll see what I can do."
