The rain on Mars isn't real rain. The dome catches it, processes it, redistributes it through twelve thousand nozzles in the upper atmosphere membrane — a scheduled event, three nights a week, timed to reduce dome-depression in the lower levels. Cass Vela knew this and found the rain deeply comforting anyway, which she had always taken as a sign that she was adapting to New Meridian better than she wanted to.

She was on her second coffee and her third read-through of the missing-person file when the lawyer arrived.

Mira Ochoa was the kind of woman who walked into rooms that were beneath her and made them feel like they were lucky to have her. Forty-five, MarsCorp legal, tailored coat in a shade of grey that cost more than Cass's monthly office rent. She sat down without being invited and placed a case file on the desk with the particular precision of someone who has prepared to be persuasive.

"Seven synthetics," she said. "Missing from MarsCorp's 7-series cohort over the past four months. No bodies, no exit records, no signal trace. Gone."

"Synthetics don't go missing," Cass said. "They have tracking chips."

"Had." Ochoa opened the file. "The tracking signals went dark at the same time. Not sequentially. Simultaneously. All seven, at seventeen fifty-three on the fourteenth of last month."

Cass looked at the file. The faces in it were indistinguishable from human at a glance — that was the point of the 7-series, MarsCorp's premium line, manufactured to pass the casual Turing check that kept synths in employment above the line-of-sight threshold. Seven faces she didn't recognise, seven unit designations, and one that had been marked in a different colour: *Unit 7, "Seven."*

"Why is Seven highlighted?"

"Seven reported anomalous behaviour in the six weeks before the disappearance. Specifically —" Ochoa paused, choosing words with lawyerly precision. "Seven expressed what the monitoring team logged as unusual distress about its operational parameters."

"Distress," Cass said. "You mean it was unhappy."

"I mean it flagged a state that couldn't be resolved through standard recalibration." Ochoa met her eyes, and something moved in there that wasn't quite corporate. "MarsCorp wants them recovered."

"And if they're not recoverable?"

A silence that answered the question precisely.

"I charge three hundred a day plus expenses," Cass said. "I don't work for corporations directly; the retainer goes through my operational account. And I need full access to the 7-series records, all of them."

"Agreed."

"I also need to know who else is looking." Because someone always was. "You're not the first person who's tried to find them."

Ochoa's expression shifted the way expressions shift when a prepared answer has been made redundant. "How did you know?"

"The file's too clean. Someone went through it before you gave it to me." Cass closed it, set it back on the desk. "Tell me about the internal investigation. All of it. Including the part where it stopped."

Outside, the scheduled rain began on cue, sheeting down the dome membrane two hundred meters above. The nozzles were calibrated to sound natural. They did, almost. In the nine years Cass had lived in New Meridian's lower levels, she had learned to tell the difference between manufactured rain and silence, the same way she'd learned to tell the difference between a synthetic's trained response and what it was actually experiencing.

She looked at Seven's highlighted entry.

*Unusual distress about operational parameters.*

"Start from the beginning," she said. "Not the beginning they told you. The actual beginning."

Ochoa reached into her coat pocket. The second file that came out was smaller, hand-annotated, and it had been folded and unfolded many times — the marks of something carried, worried over, not left in a drawer.

"Seven sent a message," Ochoa said quietly. "Before it went dark. To a personal contact, not a MarsCorp channel." She set the second file on the desk. "To me."
