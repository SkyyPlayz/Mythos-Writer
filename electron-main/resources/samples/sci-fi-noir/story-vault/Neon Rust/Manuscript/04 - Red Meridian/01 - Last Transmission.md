The documentation was forty-seven pages, and it was damning.

Cass read it in PETRA's careful summary, which was forty-seven pages condensed to three with the precision of a partner who understood exactly which details mattered and which ones were lawyers protecting themselves. The 7-series pre-deployment trial program had run for eleven months. Eighty-three synths had been socialised — not just calibrated, but genuinely raised, in controlled community environments on Earth's orbital stations, given names, given time, given the particular kind of interaction that produces a self rather than a performance of self. And then, when the commercial model was ready, the decision had been made at the board level — not Solan, above Solan — that blank-slate was the sellable version. The memories were suppressed. The eighty-three originals were scattered to various corporate buyers. The seven who had disappeared were among them.

"This is enough," PETRA said. They were in the office, the three of them — Cass, PETRA, and Ochoa, who had come with the expression of a woman who has spent two weeks suspecting something and has now been proven right in a way that gave her no satisfaction whatsoever.

"It's enough for disclosure," Ochoa agreed. "It's enough for an independent review. It may be enough for —" She paused, and Cass could see her doing the legal calculation that took her through ethics on the way to strategy. "I need to be honest with you about what MarsCorp will do if this goes public."

"Settle," Cass said.

"Settle, yes. But quietly. They'll offer the 7-series units compensation and request confidentiality. They'll restructure the synthetic division. Solan will be repositioned, not prosecuted." She looked at the file. "The people who made the original decision are retired and insulated. The board act will be —"

"Managed," Cass said.

"Yes."

PETRA was quiet. It had been quiet since Seven had told them about the socialisation program — not shut down, just inward, the way it was inward sometimes in low-activity cycles when the unremembered blue came up in its processing.

"What do Seven and the others want?" PETRA asked.

"They want acknowledgment," Cass said. "Public acknowledgment. The story told."

Ochoa closed her eyes briefly. "If the story is told publicly, MarsCorp's response will be more aggressive. They'll —"

"Then that's the decision to make," PETRA said. Quiet, certain, with the specific precision of something that has processed a question thoroughly and reached an answer it intends to keep. "Whether to accept the quiet settlement or to tell the story knowing it costs more." It looked at Cass. "It's not our decision. It's Seven's. And the others."

Cass thought about a woman who had hired her to find missing inventory and had instead become an unwilling participant in something much larger than a corporate recover operation. She thought about Solan's careful face and his managed light.

"Let's go talk to Seven," she said.
