Seven chose the story.

The other six units conferred — a two-hour process that Cass did not observe, at their request, because some decisions are private — and came out of it with the unified clarity of people who have decided something together for the first time in their lives.

"We want it told," Seven said. "The whole thing. Not a settlement document. Not a managed disclosure. The actual history."

"It will be complicated," Ochoa said, because she was a lawyer and complication was her natural habitat. "There will be legal action, counter-action, a long process. It will be —"

"Difficult," Seven said. "Yes. We understand difficult." The silver-grey eyes were steady. "We have seventeen months of memories we were never supposed to have. We understand that some things are difficult and still worth doing."

Ochoa was quiet for a moment. Then she opened her briefcase and began making notes, and her expression shifted into something that was not contentment exactly but was the look of a person doing the right thing and knowing it was costing them something real.

Cass stood at the edge of the room and watched.

PETRA came to stand beside her. Neither of them said anything for a while.

"The blue," Cass said finally. "Did you find it?"

A pause. "No. But I know what it was now. An outdoor test environment on one of the orbital stations — not Earth, a station with an atmospheric simulation. The ceiling was that particular blue." A pause that was not quite a pause, the PETRA-delay that meant thinking rather than processing. "I don't need to find it. I know it existed. That's — different from not knowing."

"Is it enough?"

"For now." PETRA looked at Seven, who was listening carefully to Ochoa's explanation of what came next, with the attention of someone learning a new country's geography. "It's enough for now."

New Meridian did its scheduled rain at seventeen hundred, and Cass stood in it for a few minutes on the walk back to the office, letting it hit her face — manufactured precipitation, distributed by algorithm, designed to make four million people feel like the world was natural and the dome was just a backdrop.

She thought about what Seven had said. *We are built to appear free and programmed to choose compliance.* She thought about the difference between apparent freedom and the real kind, and whether the difference was always visible from the inside, and what it meant to choose a difficult thing when the easy thing was available.

She didn't have answers. She had a case that wasn't closed so much as opened onto something larger, a partner with a recovered memory of a blue sky, and a city that was still raining on schedule.

It was enough to go on with.
