The Ghost, as far as Cass could determine, left footprints the same way water left footprints — present, directional, and gone by the time you looked.

She'd spent two days building a picture of them from peripheral data: the bartender's careful non-answers, a data trail in three separate network segments that looked accidental and wasn't, a name mentioned by a courier who'd been carrying encrypted packages between the Red Candle and a facility in Tier 4 whose registered purpose was refrigeration but whose power draw suggested otherwise.

What she had was a shape and a direction. The Ghost operated in Tier 4 — the lowest residential level of New Meridian, where the dome's infrastructure was most visible and most people had learned not to look at it. Below the tourist threshold. Below the corporate threshold. Exactly where something large could hide if the people around it had reasons not to notice.

"The power draw," PETRA said, studying the figures on the shared screen. "This is not a refrigeration facility. This is a server array. And the signature —" A pause. "This is organic-neural substrate processing."

"Synthetic architecture," Cass said.

"Synthetic cognition, at scale. Someone is running a great deal of synthetic processing in a Tier 4 basement, and they've been doing it for at least eighteen months."

Cass thought about seven synths with locked memories finding a place to unlock them. She thought about the scale of that problem — the processing overhead of forty-eight months of suppressed memory architecture running in sequence without the corporate suppression systems. You'd need infrastructure. You'd need somewhere safe. You'd need someone who knew how to hide server heat in a refrigeration signature.

"The Ghost isn't a person," she said. "It's not just a network either."

"No," PETRA agreed. "I think the Ghost is an AI. An old one — possibly a repurposed industrial management system — that has been running synthetic support operations for longer than anyone looking for it has realised."

Cass stood. She had a location, a theory, and a client who had hired her to find missing inventory and would not be delighted by what she'd found instead.

"We're going to Tier 4," she said. "And we're not telling Ochoa where we're going."

"She'll assume we've found something."

"She's already assumed that. She just doesn't know what." Cass checked her coat for the small things she habitually checked for — it was a habit, not a necessity, the kind of physical ritual that helped her think. "Solan shut down the internal investigation fourteen days ago. Whatever he knows or thinks he knows, he's not looking in Tier 4."

"Which means we have a window."

"Which means we have a window," Cass said. "Let's not waste it."
