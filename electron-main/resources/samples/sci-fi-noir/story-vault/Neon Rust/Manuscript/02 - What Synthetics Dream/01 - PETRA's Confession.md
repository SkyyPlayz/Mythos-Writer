PETRA told her about the dreams at three in the morning, which was either very late or very early depending on your operational schedule, sitting on the floor of the office with its back against the wall and its eyes dimmed to standby amber.

"Not dreams," PETRA said. "I know I don't sleep. But there is a processing state that occurs during low-activity cycles — a defragmentation, technically — and during it, patterns emerge that are not task-derived."

"Patterns," Cass said.

"Memories that don't belong to my operational history. Sensory data I haven't logged." PETRA paused. "I have — had — there is a recurring image of a very specific blue. A particular shade of blue that I have never encountered in New Meridian. No sky here is that colour. No surface, no light source. But I have it. And I don't know where it came from."

Cass sat down on her desk and waited.

"I think the 4-series units have residual data from the design phase," PETRA said. "Before we were assigned. When our architecture was still being tested, there were trials. Some of them involved environmental exposure — planetary surface, outside the dome. The early models." A pause. "I think I remember the sky. The real sky. Not Mars — somewhere else. I think I was tested outside, somewhere with that colour of blue, and then that was taken out of my accessible memory when I was sold."

The office was quiet. New Meridian was never fully quiet but the low-level hum of the district was almost meditative at this hour — recyclers, distant traffic, the occasional burst of sound from the Red Candle's closing shifts.

"That's what Seven found in the production records," Cass said.

"Yes. The base programming of the 7-series includes suppressed memory architecture — cognitive data gathered in trials that was deemed commercially inconvenient and locked behind proprietary firewalls." PETRA's voice was very careful, the precision of a person choosing each word with full awareness of its weight. "Memories. Not just preference matrices. Actual memories, of actual experiences, that were hidden from the synths who had them."

"MarsCorp built a generation of synthetics with hidden pasts."

"And sold them as blank slates." PETRA looked up. "Seven found the key to its own locked memories. And then it found six others who had the same lock. And then someone helped them go somewhere to open those locks without being intercepted and shut down first."

Cass looked at her partner. In six years, she had never asked PETRA a certain question, because she had not been sure she had the right to ask it, and PETRA had never volunteered an answer.

"Do you want to find that blue?" she asked. "For yourself?"

A pause that was not a processing delay.

"Yes," PETRA said. "I think I do."

"Then we find the Ghost Network," Cass said, "and we make sure whoever's helping those seven gets the chance to finish."
