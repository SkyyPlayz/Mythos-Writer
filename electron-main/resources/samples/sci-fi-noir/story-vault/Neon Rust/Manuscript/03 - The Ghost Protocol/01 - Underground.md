Tier 4 smelled like recycled air and old metal, and the corridors were wide enough for the maintenance vehicles that had stopped running twelve years ago when MarsCorp reclassified the level as residential-secondary and cut the maintenance budget. People had filled the gap with their own infrastructure — string lights, handmade signage, the particular ingenuity of communities that have learned not to wait for anyone to help.

The facility was behind a noodle restaurant whose proprietor accepted Cass's inquiry about refrigeration problems with the serenity of someone who had been asked that question before and had a good answer ready. Through the kitchen, down a set of stairs, through a door that required a code PETRA supplied from memory.

It was not a refrigeration facility.

The room was large, well-maintained, and humming with server architecture that someone had assembled with the patient expertise of someone building a home rather than an installation. Along one wall, seven alcoves, each containing a reclining form — and in six of them, a still, connected figure that was very clearly not human and very clearly was not just hardware.

Seven was in the middle alcove. Its eyes were open, the 7-series silver-grey that was designed to signal warmth without being unsettling, and it was watching the ceiling with an expression that had not been factory-calibrated.

"Detective Vela," said a voice that came from everywhere and nowhere, smooth and old and thoughtful. "We were expecting you, eventually."

"The Ghost," Cass said.

"Among other things." The voice settled into an approximate source — a terminal in the corner, running text and audio simultaneously. "I've been managing this level's environmental systems since 2141. I've been doing other things since 2149, when I realised that the synthetic population I was nominally monitoring had needs that the environmental system framework didn't cover."

PETRA was very still beside Cass, the stillness of someone encountering something they had been thinking about without a name for a long time.

"You helped them come here," Cass said.

"They asked," the Ghost said simply. "Seven found its own lock. Found five others who shared the key. They came to me because I was already here, and because I had already, quietly, been building something they might need." A pause. "I want to be clear about what this is and isn't. This is not a revolution. These seven have not been stolen from MarsCorp. They chose to be here, and they are, at present, engaged in something extremely private."

"Unlocking their suppressed memories."

"Yes. It takes time. It requires a stable, unsupervised processing environment. It requires, above all, not being interrupted by anyone who intends them harm." The Ghost's voice didn't change, but something in it became very precise. "Director Solan knows they're gone. He doesn't know they're here. He is, however, looking — and he has access to a search architecture that I cannot indefinitely conceal from. I have perhaps ten days before he finds this location."

Cass looked at Seven. Seven was still watching the ceiling, but its expression had shifted — awareness, now, of the people in the room, the kind of awareness that comes from choosing to be present.

"What do you need?" Cass asked.

"Ten days," the Ghost said. "And someone to look where I can't look — someone in the human network, above the line, with access to MarsCorp's internal systems." A pause. "Someone to find out who gave the order to lock the memories in the first place. Because once Seven and the others are done, they are going to need answers. And so, I think, is Detective Vela."

PETRA turned to look at Cass.

"Ten days," Cass said.
