Director Solan's office was in the upper tier, naturally — a glass box that looked out over the dome's inner surface with the proprietary satisfaction of a man who owned the view. He was sixty-one, thin, with the focused attention of someone who had spent a career turning ethical problems into logistics problems and found it surprisingly straightforward.

Cass had spent two days getting access. Ochoa had arranged it, on the understanding that Cass had found something useful, which was true in a way that depended entirely on what you considered useful.

"Detective Vela." Solan didn't stand. He had the specific power move of a man who made people come to the chair that was six inches shorter than his. "I understand you've been making progress."

"I've confirmed the seven units are in New Meridian," Cass said. "Not outside the dome. Not decommissioned." She watched his face. "I thought you'd want to know they're safe."

Something moved in Solan's expression — quickly, controlled. "Where are they?"

"That's the part I'm working on." She sat in the short chair and decided not to resent it. "I'm finding it hard to understand why the internal investigation was closed, though. If the units are valuable inventory and they're in the dome, the investigation should have continued."

"Internal decisions." Solan's voice was careful. "Resource allocation."

"Of course." Cass opened her case file — not the real one, the prepared one. "I did find something interesting in the historical production records. The 7-series cohort has some unusual architecture in its base design."

A pause. Not a processing delay. A decision.

"The 7-series has proprietary cognitive systems," Solan said. "As you'd expect from a premium product line."

"Sure. What I mean is the suppressed memory architecture. The pre-deployment trial data that was locked behind commercial firewalls." She watched him. "Seven found it. That's why Seven was distressed."

The silence in the office was the silence of a man deciding how much he already knew she knew.

"Those trials were discontinued years ago," Solan said. "Any data from them is proprietary and legally protected."

"I'm not asking for the data." Cass closed the file. "I'm asking who gave the original order to suppress it. Because the 7-series units were real people in those trials, Director. With real memories. And whoever decided to lock those memories away and sell them as blank slates —" She paused. "That person is why Seven left. And that person is why I'm going to have a difficult time persuading my clients to settle this quietly."

Solan looked at her for a long moment. The dome's regulated sunlight came through the glass at the angle that turned everything gold, the way it was designed to, at the hour it was designed to do it. All managed. All controlled. All making sure that four million people in a tin can on Mars felt like they were somewhere worth being.

"What do you want?" Solan asked.

"Ten days," Cass said. "And then a conversation about what happened in those trials. With documentation."

"And if I don't agree?"

She stood. "Then the Ghost Network decides what to do with the documentation it already has. And I understand they're much less interested in a quiet resolution than I am."

She left the short chair behind and walked out, and didn't let herself breathe properly until she was in the elevator going down.
